The through-line

I am an environmental economist and ESG strategist whose work has always been anchored in a single question: how do we make sustainability systems actually function for the institutions and communities that need them? Not in theory. In practice, in Kenya, in 2025, with real clients and real regulatory deadlines.

My career spans field conservation, intergovernmental governance, climate finance advisory, academic research, and technology-led consultancy. That breadth is sometimes mistaken for dilettantism — it is the opposite. Each chapter built something the next one required. Field work taught me what ecological data actually looks like before it becomes a dashboard metric. UNEP taught me how policy is made and how fragile consensus is. Ilkerin taught me what it takes to move an institution. Building Ardena is where all of it converges.

The work I care most about sits at the intersection of rigorous analysis and practical application — producing tools, frameworks, and advisory that change how organizations engage with environmental risk and opportunity, not just how they report on it.

On East Africa specifically

Kenya is at a genuinely interesting moment in the global sustainability transition. The 2027 mandatory IFRS S1/S2 disclosure deadline for financial institutions and listed companies is approaching, and the infrastructure to meet it — the advisory capacity, the data systems, the trained practitioners — is still being built. The Climate Change (Carbon Markets) Regulations 2024 created one of Africa's most sophisticated carbon market frameworks almost overnight, yet most of the financial sector still does not understand what it requires of them.

These are the gaps I work in. The ones where good analysis, translated into usable tools and clear advisory, can compress years of uncertainty into actionable decisions. That is the work Ardena was founded to do, and it is the work that gives coherence to everything in my background.
