Kenya Carbon Project
Benefit-Sharing Calculator

Kenya's Climate Change (Carbon Markets) Regulations, 2024 — Legal Notice No. 84 — are among the most detailed carbon market frameworks on the continent, and almost nobody had translated them into a usable decision-support tool. This calculator does exactly that. It computes the full financial obligations for any carbon project operating in Kenya: mandatory community benefit-sharing (differentiated by land type), Climate Change Fund levies, NEMA issuance fees, and net developer revenue — all live, all in a single interface.

What makes this technically significant is the Article 6.2 layer. The tool includes complete logic for internationally transferred mitigation outcomes (ITMOs) — the corresponding adjustment fee structure (USD 4.00/ITMO), its remittance split to the Climate Change Fund, and contextual information on Kenya's active bilateral agreements with Switzerland and Sweden, as well as the GGGI-supported pipeline with Norway, Singapore, and New Zealand. This is the level of market mechanics that separates generalist ESG advisors from genuine carbon market practitioners.

Building this required not just reading the regulations, but understanding how they interact — land classification rules, proponent nationality schedules, fee hierarchies, and the distinction between domestic voluntary markets and Article 6.2 authorization pathways. For any institution entering Kenya's carbon market, this tool shortens weeks of legal review into minutes.

Demonstrates: Carbon market regulation literacy · Article 6 mechanics · Legal instrument translation · Tool design for practitioners

Kenya GHG
Emissions Dashboard

Kenya publishes greenhouse gas inventory data — but it lives in PDFs, annexes, and technical reports that are largely inaccessible to the decision-makers who need them most: investors, development finance institutions, policy teams, and corporate sustainability leads assessing country-level climate exposure and opportunity. This dashboard was built to close that gap.

The work involved sourcing, cleaning, and structuring Kenya's national GHG data across five sectors — energy, agriculture, land use and forestry (LULUCF), waste, and industrial processes — then designing visualizations that reveal the structural story: which sectors dominate emissions, how trajectories have shifted over time, where mitigation potential concentrates, and how Kenya's emissions profile compares to NDC commitments and regional peers.

This is MRV (measurement, reporting, and verification) applied as analytical infrastructure. The ability to take raw national inventory data and turn it into something a CFO or an Article 6.2 buyer can use in a conversation is a specific and undervalued skill in East African climate finance — and one that directly supports investment due diligence, carbon project siting, and NDC-aligned portfolio strategy.

Demonstrates: GHG accounting & national inventory analysis · MRV systems thinking · Data storytelling for non-technical audiences · Climate finance due diligence support

Area Neutrality: Safeguarding Urban Biodiversity in Trondheim

Published in IOP Environmental Science, this co-authored research tackled a deceptively difficult policy problem: how do you allow a city to grow without systematically eroding the ecological fabric that makes it liveable? The paper proposes an area neutrality framework — a land management approach that requires development to offset ecological losses through restoration or conservation elsewhere, creating a functional balance sheet for urban biodiversity.

The research was conducted in Trondheim, Norway, in collaboration with the Sustainability Centre, and grounded in real municipal planning data. It required synthesizing international environmental law, urban planning theory, ecological monitoring principles, and local governance constraints into a framework with practical application — not just theoretical elegance.

The policy logic in this paper translates directly to ESG and nature-related disclosure work. The conceptual architecture — baseline assessment, impact accounting, mitigation hierarchy, and offset design — maps closely onto TNFD (Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures) and biodiversity net gain frameworks now entering corporate reporting requirements globally. This is the kind of foundational thinking that informs how I approach nature-related risk advisory and ecosystem service valuation.

Demonstrates: Environmental policy design · Biodiversity & land use accounting · Academic research to practical framework translation · Nature-related disclosure thinking

Regreening Africa: A Bottom-up Transformation of Degraded Lands

Contributing author to this ETFRN (European Tropical Forest Research Network) publication, produced in collaboration with World Agroforestry (ICRAF) as part of the Regreening Africa programme — one of the largest landscape restoration initiatives on the continent, operating across Ethiopia, Ghana, Kenya, Mali, Niger, Rwanda, and Senegal.

The publication examines farmer-managed natural regeneration and community-led land restoration approaches that have transformed degraded dryland landscapes across Sub-Saharan Africa — not through top-down intervention, but through enabling the conditions for natural recovery and local stewardship. My contribution drew on stakeholder engagement and ecosystem research from the field, translating field observations and data into the kind of structured, evidence-based analysis that informs restoration programme design.

This work is directly relevant to carbon project development. Land-based carbon projects — afforestation, reforestation, improved land management — depend on exactly the kind of community engagement, baseline ecological assessment, and long-term monitoring capacity that this research was built around. Understanding what landscape restoration actually looks like on the ground is what separates credible carbon project developers from those who mistake paper methodologies for field reality.

Demonstrates: Field research & ecosystem assessment · Community-based conservation programme design · Land-based carbon project grounding · Multi-country programme analysis
