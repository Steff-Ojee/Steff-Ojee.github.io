Kenya Carbon Project
Benefit-Sharing Calculator

Kenya's Climate Change (Carbon Markets) Regulations, 2024 — Legal Notice No. 84 — are among the most detailed carbon market frameworks on the continent, and almost nobody had translated them into a usable decision-support tool. This calculator does exactly that: it computes the full financial obligations for any carbon project operating in Kenya — mandatory community benefit-sharing (differentiated by land type and proponent nationality), Climate Change Fund levies, NEMA issuance fees, and net developer revenue — all live, in a single interface.

What makes this technically significant is the Article 6.2 layer. The tool includes complete logic for internationally transferred mitigation outcomes (ITMOs) — the corresponding adjustment fee structure, its remittance split to the Climate Change Fund, and contextual information on Kenya's active bilateral agreements with Switzerland and Sweden, as well as the GGGI-supported pipeline with Norway, Singapore, and New Zealand.

Building this required not just reading the regulations, but understanding how they interact — land classification rules, proponent nationality schedules, fee hierarchies, and the distinction between domestic voluntary markets and Article 6.2 authorization pathways. This is the level of market mechanics that separates generalist ESG advisors from genuine carbon market practitioners. For any institution entering Kenya's carbon market, this tool shortens weeks of legal review into minutes.

Kenya GHG
Emissions Dashboard

Kenya publishes greenhouse gas inventory data — but it lives in PDFs, annexes, and technical reports that are largely inaccessible to the decision-makers who need them most: investors, development finance institutions, policy teams, and corporate sustainability leads assessing country-level climate exposure and opportunity. This dashboard was built to close that gap.

The work involved sourcing, cleaning, and structuring Kenya's national GHG data across five sectors — energy, agriculture, land use and forestry (LULUCF), waste, and industrial processes — then designing visualizations that reveal the structural story: which sectors dominate emissions, how trajectories have shifted over time, where mitigation potential concentrates, and how Kenya's emissions profile compares to NDC commitments.

This is MRV applied as analytical infrastructure. The ability to take raw national inventory data and turn it into something a CFO or an Article 6.2 buyer can use in a conversation is a specific and undervalued skill in East African climate finance — one that directly supports investment due diligence, carbon project siting, and NDC-aligned portfolio strategy.

Area Neutrality: Safeguarding
Urban Biodiversity in Trondheim

Published in IOP Environmental Science, this co-authored research tackled a deceptively difficult policy problem: how do you allow a city to grow without systematically eroding the ecological fabric that makes it liveable? The paper proposes an area neutrality framework — a land management approach that requires development to offset ecological losses through restoration or conservation elsewhere, creating a functional balance sheet for urban biodiversity.

The research was grounded in real municipal planning data from Trondheim, Norway, and required synthesizing international environmental law, urban planning theory, ecological monitoring principles, and local governance constraints into a framework with practical application — not just theoretical elegance.

The policy logic here translates directly to nature-related disclosure work. The conceptual architecture — baseline assessment, impact accounting, mitigation hierarchy, and offset design — maps closely onto TNFD and biodiversity net gain frameworks now entering corporate reporting requirements globally.
Demonstrates: Environmental policy design Biodiversity accounting Research-to-framework translation Nature-related disclosure thinking

Regreening Africa: A Bottom-up
Transformation of Degraded Lands

Contributing author to this ETFRN publication, produced with World Agroforestry (ICRAF) as part of the Regreening Africa programme — one of the largest landscape restoration initiatives on the continent, operating across Ethiopia, Ghana, Kenya, Mali, Niger, Rwanda, and Senegal.

The publication examines farmer-managed natural regeneration and community-led land restoration approaches that have transformed degraded dryland landscapes across Sub-Saharan Africa — not through top-down intervention, but through enabling the conditions for natural recovery and local stewardship. My contribution drew on stakeholder engagement and ecosystem research from the field, translating observations and data into structured, evidence-based analysis that informs restoration programme design.

Land-based carbon projects — afforestation, reforestation, improved land management — depend on exactly the kind of community engagement, baseline ecological assessment, and long-term monitoring capacity that this research was built around. Understanding what landscape restoration actually looks like on the ground is what separates credible carbon project developers from those who mistake paper methodologies for field reality.

Ardena ESG Platform —
AI-Powered Compliance Infrastructure for East Africa

Most ESG advisory firms sell time and expertise. Ardena was built to sell infrastructure — a platform that automates the analytical, data, and reporting workflows that would otherwise consume the majority of a sustainability team's capacity. As CTO and Co-Founder, designing and building this platform is the most technically complex work I have done, and the most direct expression of what I believe about how ESG advisory needs to evolve in East Africa.

The platform is not a generic ESG tool. It is built specifically for the regulatory and data environment of East Africa — calibrated to Kenya's 2027 IFRS S1/S2 compliance requirements, structured around the data maturity of mid-market financial institutions, and designed to produce disclosure-ready outputs rather than advisory reports that need a consultant to interpret.

The core modules address the four most resource-intensive steps in ESG compliance for a typical Kenyan financial institution. The ESG data input and reporting automation layer replaces the spreadsheet-and-email workflows that most institutions currently use for sustainability data collection, replacing them with structured input flows that feed directly into standardized reporting templates. The carbon inventory calculation engine applies GHG Protocol methodology to the specific asset classes, operational footprints, and financed emissions profiles of East African financial sector clients — not a generic Scope 1/2/3 calculator, but one calibrated to the sector's actual data availability and the region's emissions factors.

The IFRS S1/S2 gap analysis module is perhaps the most operationally significant. It maps a client's current governance structures, risk identification processes, metrics, and disclosures against the specific requirements of IFRS S1 (general sustainability disclosures) and IFRS S2 (climate-related disclosures), producing a prioritized gap register with remediation guidance. For a financial institution beginning their compliance journey in 2025 with a 2027 deadline, this turns a six-week consulting engagement into a structured, repeatable self-assessment. The document and disclosure generation layer then takes the structured data produced by the other modules and generates IFRS-aligned disclosure drafts, reducing the time from data to submission-ready document by an order of magnitude.

The platform's intelligence layer is where domain expertise and technology meet most directly. Its document intelligence capability processes regulatory filings, existing sustainability reports, and financial disclosures to extract structured, comparable data — turning documents that would take a skilled analyst days to work through into structured inputs that drive the rest of the platform. The disclosure generation module applies that analysis to produce IFRS S1/S2-aligned draft outputs calibrated to each client's specific governance structures, materiality profile, and data environment — not generic templates, but drafts that reflect where the client actually is. The compliance chat assistant allows clients to interrogate their own compliance position in natural language: asking which gaps carry the highest regulatory risk, what a specific IFRS requirement means for their sector, or how to interpret a materiality assessment result. The answers are grounded in their actual data and position, not boilerplate guidance.

Building these features required the domain knowledge to define what correct looks like — knowing what IFRS S1/S2 requires at the level of individual disclosure requirements, how materiality is assessed across different financial institution types, what Kenyan regulatory language means in practice, and where ambiguities exist that a system should flag rather than resolve. The technology operationalizes that expertise. It does not replace it.
