2025 — Present
Ardena Consulting Limited
Nairobi, Kenya
ESG Advisory

Building the advisory infrastructure
Kenya's ESG transition actually needs

Ardena Consulting was founded in January 2025 on a specific thesis: Kenya's financial institutions and listed companies were approaching a mandatory compliance deadline — IFRS S1/S2 by 2027 — with almost no advisory infrastructure designed specifically for East African regulatory realities. The frameworks existed. The expertise to implement them locally did not, at least not at scale.

The work of building Ardena has been the work of proving that thesis wrong by filling the gap ourselves. Within six months of founding, we moved from concept to active client engagement — developing ESMS implementation frameworks aligned with IFC Performance Standards, building ESG training curricula covering Kenya, Rwanda, and Tanzania regulatory contexts simultaneously, and positioning the firm at the intersection of AI tooling and sustainability advisory.

The question we return to with every client engagement is the same: what would it actually take for this institution to meet its sustainability obligations not just on paper, but in practice? The answer is almost always more specific, more operationally grounded, and more attentive to local context than the standard advisory frameworks suggest.

The technical architecture behind Ardena's advisory draws on the full stack of disclosure frameworks — IFRS S1/S2, TCFD, GRI, IFC Performance Standards — but the real differentiator is implementation depth. Designing an ESMS is not the same as designing one that a Nairobi-based financial institution's compliance team can actually run. That gap — between framework and function — is where Ardena works.

Oct 2024 — May 2025
United Nations Environment Programme
Nairobi, Kenya
Governance

At the centre of global environmental governance — how it actually works

UNEP's Nairobi headquarters is where the formal architecture of global environmental governance becomes visible. Sitting in that environment — facilitating engagement between 9 Major Groups and 193 Member State representatives, coordinating Group of Friends consultations, supporting the preparatory processes for UNEA-7 — gives you an understanding of multilateral systems that no amount of external study can replicate.

The governance affairs work was substantive. Civil society access to intergovernmental processes is structurally constrained in ways that are rarely discussed openly, and much of the coordination work was about building the conditions under which genuine engagement could happen — not just formal participation. The INC Plastic Pollution stakeholder accreditation process, which I helped streamline across 5 UN regional groups, is a concrete example of where procedural work has direct consequences for who gets to speak in rooms where environmental commitments are made.

Global environmental governance is built on the gap between what countries commit to in negotiating rooms and what they implement at home. Understanding that gap — where it comes from, how it functions, and where it can be closed — is foundational to any serious climate advisory practice.

The analytical work ran in parallel: producing quantitative analysis and data visualizations that informed executive-level strategy decisions and stakeholder engagement protocols. This is the kind of analysis that has to be both technically defensible and politically readable — a combination that demands careful calibration of what you present and how.

Jan — Oct 2024
Ilkerin Consulting
Nairobi, Kenya
ESG · Capacity Building

What it actually takes to move an institution toward sustainability

ESG advisory at the institutional level is not primarily a technical challenge — it is an organizational change challenge in which technical knowledge is a necessary but insufficient condition. The work at Ilkerin made this clear very quickly.

Delivering ESG capacity building programmes to 100+ professionals across banking, manufacturing, and agricultural sectors means navigating the full spectrum of institutional resistance: the CFO who understands that ESG reporting is coming but sees it as a compliance cost rather than a business opportunity; the sustainability team that has the motivation but not the technical grounding; the C-suite that has committed publicly to carbon neutrality without a credible pathway to achieve it. Each requires a different kind of engagement.

The roadmaps I developed for client organizations were designed to make the pathway from baseline carbon inventory to measurable reduction target concrete enough to act on — not aspirational documents that live in a sustainability report, but operational plans with owners, timelines, and data requirements. Achieving 95% participant satisfaction ratings across these programmes suggests that the approach — technically rigorous, practically oriented, and honest about difficulty — resonates.

2023
OnePointFive Academy
Remote / Global
Climate Finance

Deepening the technical foundation
of climate finance practice

The OnePointFive Academy fellowship is not a general sustainability programme — it is an 8-week intensive designed to build practitioner-grade expertise in climate finance and carbon markets, and the cohort of 500+ professionals from 40+ countries reflects that ambition. Being selected from a competitive global pool for this programme was meaningful precisely because of the specificity of what it offers.

The technical depth covered here — Net Zero transition planning, GHG Protocol implementation, SBTi target-setting methodology, TCFD, CDP, and ISSB disclosure frameworks — maps almost exactly onto the skills that institutions preparing for Kenya's 2027 IFRS S1/S2 compliance deadline need from their advisors. This was not coincidental curriculum design; these are the frameworks that have become the operating standards of global corporate sustainability.

The quantitative approaches to carbon credit valuation and additionality assessment developed during this fellowship — working alongside practitioners from development finance institutions, major corporates, and specialized climate funds — built the analytical confidence that makes the difference between knowing what a framework requires and knowing whether a specific project actually meets it.

Consulting · Events

The Norwegian chapter: research, production, and a different environmental context

The two years in Trondheim, completing an MSc at NTNU, were not just academic — they were a deliberate exposure to how sustainability is practiced in a different institutional and regulatory environment. Norway's approach to environmental governance, urban planning, and corporate responsibility operates from different premises than Kenya's, and understanding both is what makes the comparative analysis I do credible.

Creating and delivering sustainability training curricula during UN Trade Week was an early test of whether I could translate technical environmental knowledge into something that non-specialist audiences could act on. The answer, it turned out, was yes, with significant effort toward clarity and framing.

TEDx Trondheim was a different kind of work entirely — managing the complete production lifecycle for 5 thematic event sessions spanning innovation, technology, and social impact. Partnership cultivation, logistics, stakeholder coordination under time pressure. These are skills that do not appear in most sustainability CVs, but they are the operational scaffolding that makes everything else possible.

2020 — 2021

Kenya
Conservation · Field

Field conservation in Kenya's arid lands — where data meets the ground

The work at RAE Ltd was field work in the proper sense — community-based conservation programmes across arid and semi-arid land sites, direct engagement with local stakeholders on climate resilience initiatives, GIS mapping that covered 100% of project intervention areas. This is where environmental data acquires its meaning: not as numbers in a report, but as information grounded in specific landscapes, specific communities, and specific ecological conditions.

The GIS and spatial analysis work here built the technical foundation for everything I have done with environmental data since. Establishing baseline spatial data for monitoring and evaluation across multiple field sites — while also deploying a digital knowledge-sharing platform that improved cross-team coordination efficiency by 50% — required holding the technical and the operational dimensions of a programme simultaneously.

The relationship-building with government authorities that informed land restoration policy development is a less visible but equally significant part of this period. Bridging community needs with national environmental priorities is a specific kind of translation work, and it taught me a great deal about how policy actually gets made — or doesn't — at the intersection of community reality and government process.

Research · Communications

to write for a global audience

Ministry of Agriculture, Livestock, Irrigation & Fisheries
Nairobi, Kenya
Policy · Agriculture

An early lesson in how government policy is actually made

Working inside a government ministry as a student is a particular kind of education — one in the gap between policy intention and institutional reality. At the Ministry of Agriculture, Livestock, Irrigation and Fisheries, I was not filing paperwork. You learn to be accountable to evidence, not just to effort.

Jun — Sep 2017
Lafarge EcoSystems (Bamburi Cement)
Mombasa, Kenya
Biodiversity · Rehabilitation

Haller Park and the concrete possibility of ecological recovery

Haller Park — formerly a quarried limestone wasteland, today one of Kenya's most visited wildlife sanctuaries — is perhaps the most compelling proof of concept for ecological restoration in this country. To have worked there, at the beginning of a career in environmental sustainability, was to have that career grounded in something real: not a framework, not a report, but a place that had been broken and rebuilt.

The work was precise and biological. Breeding three butterfly species for introduction into the Butterfly Pavilion required understanding the specific conditions each species needed — the host plants, the humidity, the sequencing of life stages. Maintaining species catalogues, monitoring wildlife sanctuaries, participating in the transformation of the degraded quarry floor into planted butterfly habitat: this is what ecosystem restoration looks like at the level of a single organism, which is ultimately the only level at which it is real.

Delivering educational tours to local and international visitors was a first exercise in translating technical ecological knowledge for non-specialist audiences — explaining why a particular plant was chosen, what role a particular insect plays, how a food web reassembles in a recovering landscape. That translation skill — from scientific process to human meaning — is something I have been developing in every role since.

What Haller Park taught me, fundamentally, was that the trajectory of a degraded ecosystem can be reversed — but only if you understand the species relationships, the soil chemistry, and the long time horizons involved. I carry that lesson into every conversation about land-based carbon projects, biodiversity net gain, and nature-related financial disclosure. It is not an abstraction. I have seen it.
